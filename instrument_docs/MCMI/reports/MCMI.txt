As discussed in chapter 5, clinicians completed a rating form for each patient participating in the study as part of the MCMI-IV tryout and standardization development phases. On the rating form, clinicians were given a description of the corresponding MCMI-IV personality patterns and clinical syndromes, and were asked to identify whether the patient had been diagnosed with a corresponding DSM diagnosis (when applicable). If the patient did not have a DSM diagnosis in that area, then the clinician was asked to provide a level of dysfunction rating:

None
Minor — patient presents with only a few traits/symptoms, but none that lead to dysfunction
Moderate — patient presents with several traits/symptoms, likely affecting daily routines/activities
Severe — patient presents with many traits/symptoms, likely justifying the formal diagnosis of a clinical disorder

These ratings, in addition to establishing the prevalence rates used for creating BR scores (see chapter 5), were also used to evaluate the diagnostic efficiency of the MCMI-IV scale scores.
Sensitivity and specificity are two diagnostic validity statistics that describe how a test performs. Figure 6.1 represents the possible outcomes of comparing predictor/test results (represented by columns) and diagnostic/classification results (represented by rows). Sensitivity is computed as a ÷ (a + b) and specificity is computed as d ÷ (c + d). Sensitivity tells us the probability that someone who has the condition will test positive for it. Specificity tells us the probability that someone who does not have the condition will test negative.

Two additional diagnostic validity statistics are positive predictive power (PPP) and negative predictive power (NPP). PPP is calculated using the formula:
PPP = (a × br) ÷ [(a × br) + (1 – br) × c]
where a and c represent the frequencies indicated in Figure 6.1, and br = base rate. PPP is interpreted as the likelihood that a person with a positive test result actually has the disorder.
NPP is calculated using the formula:
NPP = [d × (1 – br)] ÷ [(d × (1 – br)) + (b × br)]
and is interpreted as the likelihood that someone with a negative test result actually does not have the disorder. A PPP = 1 indicates no false positives, while PPP = 0.5 indicates a 50% false positive rate. Similarly, an NPP = 1 indicates no false negatives, whereas an NPP = 0.5 indicates a 50% false negative rate.
Sensitivity and specificity values are independent of the base rate of an underlying disorder; however, PPP and NPP are not. This means sensitivity and specificity provide overall summary statistics of how well the test can classify, even though this overall summary can be misleading for specific base rates. For example, a test might have both high sensitivity and specificity, yet still have a large false positive rate for conditions with high base rates. As such, it can be helpful to present PPP and NPP for varying prevalence rates of disorders given differences that can occur across different clinical settings (e.g., a general mental health facility, a specialized clinic for depressive disorders).
